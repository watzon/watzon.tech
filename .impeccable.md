## Design Context

### Users
Broad audience spanning recruiters, engineering peers, and potential collaborators. Recruiters are scanning for credibility and breadth of work — they expect polish and professionalism. Peers are reading blog posts, evaluating technical depth, and judging taste. Collaborators want to understand what Chris builds and how he thinks. The site needs to work for all three without pandering to any one group.

### Brand Personality
**Confident, light, enjoyable.**

The site should feel like talking to someone who's been doing this for years and doesn't need to prove it — but is genuinely fun to be around. Not stiff or corporate. Not tryhard or flashy. The confidence comes from restraint and precision, not from shouting. The lightness comes from wit, breathing room, and moments of delight — not from being insubstantial.

The goal is to impress. Not "good developer" — *great* developer, seasoned, with depth. The kind of portfolio that makes a recruiter pause and a peer nod.

### Aesthetic Direction
**Minimalist foundation with intentional moments of distinction.** The current design is clean and well-engineered but feels like it could belong to anyone. It needs a signature — something that makes it unmistakably *this* person's site without abandoning the restraint that makes it credible.

- **Theme**: Both light and dark, treated as equals. Light for the recruiters and daytime browsers; dark for peers and evening readers. Neither should feel like an afterthought.
- **What's missing**: Personality. The current site is technically sound but visually generic — Inter font, standard blue accent, uniform spacing. It needs typographic character, a more considered color story, and small details that reward attention.
- **Anti-references**: Generic developer portfolios with gradient text, glassmorphism cards, cyan-on-dark palettes, or "SaaS landing page" energy. Nothing that looks like it was generated by a prompt.
- **Colors**: No sacred colors. Open to a complete palette rethink.

### Design Principles

1. **Earned confidence** — Every design choice should feel deliberate, not default. The site should look like someone *chose* this, not like they accepted what a tool gave them. Restraint is the proof of taste.

2. **Signature over sameness** — One memorable typographic pairing, one unexpected color accent, one interaction that sticks. Better to have one unforgettable detail than ten forgettable ones.

3. **Respect the reader** — Generous whitespace, readable type, clear hierarchy. The content is the point. Design serves it, never competes with it.

4. **Light and dark as equals** — Both themes get full design attention. Dark mode isn't "invert the colors." Each theme should feel native to its context.

5. **Quiet craft** — The best details are the ones people notice on the second visit. Subtle animations, considered spacing, typographic finesse. The site should reward closer inspection without demanding it.
